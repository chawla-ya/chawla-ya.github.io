Overview

Built a visual SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) system that uses AprilTag fiducial markers as landmarks. The system leverages factor graph optimization via the GTSAM library to jointly estimate camera poses and landmark positions from sequential image observations.

Problem

A mobile robot navigating an environment needs to simultaneously build a map of its surroundings and localize itself within that map. Odometry alone accumulates drift over time, making it unreliable for long trajectories. Visual landmarks provide loop closure opportunities to correct this drift, but require a robust optimization backend to fuse noisy measurements.

Approach

The system detects AprilTags in each camera frame and extracts their relative pose using the known tag geometry and calibrated camera intrinsics. These measurements are encoded as factors in a factor graph, along with odometry factors between consecutive poses.

GTSAM's nonlinear optimizer (Levenberg-Marquardt) solves the full graph to find the maximum a posteriori estimate of all robot poses and landmark positions simultaneously. The factor graph formulation naturally handles the sparse structure of the problem and scales efficiently with trajectory length.

Key Features

Factor Graph Optimization

GTSAM-based backend for globally consistent pose estimation with loop closure support.

AprilTag Detection

Robust fiducial marker detection providing 6-DOF relative pose measurements from monocular images.

Camera Calibration

Intrinsic calibration pipeline to ensure accurate projection models for landmark observations.

Drift Correction

Landmark re-observations provide loop closures that eliminate accumulated odometry drift.

Results

The optimized trajectory showed significant improvement over raw odometry, with landmark re-observations successfully closing loops and producing globally consistent maps. The system demonstrated robust performance across varying lighting conditions and viewing angles.

What I Learned

This project gave me hands-on experience with probabilistic graphical models for robotics, the mechanics of factor graph construction and optimization, and the practical challenges of visual feature detection in real-world environments. I also deepened my understanding of camera models and SE(3) transformations.
